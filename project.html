Home Lab Journey

I am a tech enthusiast and I love to learn new things revolving the tech field.

Over the course of my last few months I have been working on various projects, my proudest one being my home lab that I have built in my dormitory.

It all started when I began simply bypassing the accessibility of my campus network. I used a router with MAC address cloning to establish a private subnet in my dormitory, which allowed me to have full control of my network traffic and routing.

I took a course last semester called Intro to OS and Networks. Throughout this course I learned about the virtualization platform called VirtualBox. This course provided the crucial foundation and understanding of Type-2 hypervisors, the logic of virtual switching, and the nuances of hardware abstraction. This enabled me to set up a virtual cyberlab to test and learn about various networking concepts like configuring a virtual firewall and routing VLANs to different virtual machines such as Kali Linux, Metasploit, and Ubuntu Server.

Running this entire stack on my primary desktop meant interference with daily use of my computer. That was when I needed dedicated compute resources. That experience sparked my interest in building my own physical home lab so that I could have a dedicated environment for testing and learning without interfering with my daily computing needs, running 24/7.

I started off by purchasing two micro-PCs: a Dell Optiplex Mini and an HP Helpdesk 800 G1. I installed Proxmox VE, allowing me to configure the nodes as a cluster with centralized management. I experimented with the 2 nodes for a while, learning about network architecture and segmentation with VLAN configuration and experimenting with a variety of services, alongside running labs like penetration testing and vulnerability assessment.

That wasn't enough for me. I eventually expanded to a three-node setup, establishing quorum using a TP-Link managed switch to segment my traffic from the router directly to nodes. I was then able to get my hands on a Dell Optiplex 980 on campus, where I installed OPNsense, transforming it into a physical firewall and router. From there I was able to move my nodes from my home network to a dedicated management VLAN through 802.1Q VLAN tagging, ensuring that management traffic and lab traffic are logically isolated. I also got my hands on a Raspberry Pi 3B+ and placed it on a dedicated VLAN. I configured AdGuard as a high-level DNS filter and Unbound as a recursive DNS resolver, eliminating reliance on third-party upstream providers like Google or Cloudflare for better privacy and security.
